\chapter{Methodology and Implementation}\label{chapter:methodology}

\section{Problem Statement and Prediction Target}

In this thesis, we address the transfer of the T3 runtime prediction approach from Umbra to PostgreSQL parsed plans.  
The central methodological goal is to preserve T3’s core modeling principles.
Upon that, we adapt plan conversion and feature extraction to PostgreSQL-specific plan information.

Also, the prediction target is total query runtime.  
Following T3, each query plan is decomposed into pipelines and a per-tuple runtime is predicted for each pipeline.
Then, we aggregate the per-tuple and pipeline runtimes to obtain the final query-runtime estimate.

\section{Data and Preprocessing}
For data collection, we use the public Zero-Shot benchmark data introduced with the Zero-Shot Cost Model \parencite{zeroshot}.  
The benchmark is particularly suitable for this thesis because it combines real-world datasets with established benchmark workloads.
It combines real-world data (e.g., IMDB with JOB queries) with synthetic benchmark data from SSB and TPC-H.
Overall, the benchmark contains 20 databases with large variation in tables, columns, and foreign-key relationships.

Having multiple databases allows us to test the generalization capabilities of our model.
Similar to the T3 paper, we can train the model on all but one databases and test on the remaining one.
Moreover as the widely used JOB workload is included, we can compare our performance predictions to other implementations later on.
Further, PostgreSQL is the intended database system of our implementation, which avoids the effort of building a separate PostgreSQL benchmark.

Following the benchmark construction described in the paper, queries are generated per database and executed on a PostgreSQL DBMS (v12) on c8220 nodes on the CloudLab platform.
Then, they are stored as raw traces (i. e., the output of \texttt{EXPLAIN ANALYZE}).
Next, the authors preprocess the queries to remove very long-running queries (queries above 30\,s are excluded) to keep the benchmark practical and reproducible \parencite{zeroshot}.
A further preprocessing step is to parse the raw traces into structured plan representations.
These parsed plans (15000 per database) contain the operator trees with node-wise attributes (e.g., operator type, estimated/actual cardinalities, widths, and runtimes).  

After preprocessing, we get plans that are similar to the Umbra plan structure, since they also consist of operator trees.
Hence, the parsed plans offer a good starting point for our pipeline and we can begin without further preprocessing.



\section{Source Model}

T3 is our source model and we transfer its core prediction logic from Umbra to PostgreSQL parsed plans.  
Hence, we adopt its three central design principles:
A pipeline-based query plan representation, tuple-centric prediction targets, and gradient-boosted decision trees for runtime prediction.
\parencite{t3}


In our implementation, we preserve these principles and adapt only representation-specific components.  
First, PostgreSQL parsed plans are converted into a T3-compatible internal structure in \texttt{src/zeroshot/zeroshot\_to\_t3.py}.  
This conversion includes operator mapping, pipeline assignment, and extraction of plan-level metadata.  
Second, model training follows tuple-centric T3 logic in \texttt{src/zeroshot/training\_zeroshot\_tpch\_holdout.py}: per-pipeline and per-tuple training data is generated, targets are transformed as $-\log(t)$, and a LightGBM model is trained with a fixed protocol.

To realize the transfer, we use two feature-design variants under the same learning setup:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Direct transfer baseline:} reuse the existing Umbra-oriented \texttt{FeatureMapper} (\texttt{src/features.py}) on converted plans.
  \item \textbf{PostgreSQL-native transfer variant:} use \texttt{PgFeatureMapper} (\texttt{src/pg\_features.py}), which computes pipeline-level vectors directly from PostgreSQL plan payload (\texttt{pg}/\texttt{plan\_parameters}) while preserving the same model interface and training flow.
\end{enumerate}

The transfer principle is therefore the following: keep T3's learning and prediction mechanics unchanged, and adapt only plan conversion and feature representation where Umbra- and PostgreSQL-specific semantics differ.
